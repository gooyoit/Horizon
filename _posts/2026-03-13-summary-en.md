## [Critical Bootloader Unlock Exploit Found in Snapdragon 8 Gen 5](https://www.ithome.com/0/928/617.htm) ⭐️ 9.0/10

A newly disclosed exploit chain, dubbed the Qualcomm GBL exploit, enables unauthorized Bootloader unlocking on devices like the Xiaomi 17 by bypassing ABL verification and downgrading SELinux enforcement via a fastboot command. The vulnerability affects Qualcomm’s latest Snapdragon 8 Gen 5 (also referred to as Snapdragon 8 Elite) SoC and has already been patched in recent HyperOS updates. This exploit undermines core Android security mechanisms, allowing full device control without manufacturer approval—bypassing warranty locks, anti-tampering measures, and potentially enabling malware persistence at the firmware level. It also challenges the assumption that modern flagship devices are secure against local privilege escalation and bootloader tampering. The exploit hinges on two flaws: (1) Qualcomm’s Android Boot Loader (ABL) fails to verify the authenticity of the Generic Bootloader (GBL) loaded from the 'efisp' partition, and (2) the fastboot oem set-gpu-preemption command lacks input validation, allowing attackers to inject 'androidboot.selinux=permissive' to disable SELinux enforcement. Once SELinux is permissive, malicious code can be written to efisp and executed during boot.

rss · IT HOME · Mar 13, 01:48

**Background**: The Bootloader is the first code executed when an Android device powers on, responsible for initializing hardware and loading the OS kernel. OEMs typically lock it to prevent unauthorized modifications, requiring official unlock procedures. Qualcomm’s ABL (Android Boot Loader) is used by many Android vendors (except Samsung, which uses its own S-Boot). SELinux (Security-Enhanced Linux) enforces mandatory access controls; in 'Enforcing' mode, it blocks unauthorized actions, but in 'Permissive' mode, it only logs violations without blocking them.

Bank deregulation in the U.S. during the 1980s and 1990s allowed banks to open more branches, changing the scale of operations. Smartphones, especially after the iPhone’s 2007 launch, enabled full-featured mobile banking apps that replicated or replaced most in-branch services.
