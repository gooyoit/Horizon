## [Scientists Achieve Vitrification and Functional Recovery of Adult Mouse Brains](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2516848123) ⭐️ 9.0/10

Researchers developed a novel cryoprotectant solution called V3 and an optimized perfusion protocol that enabled successful vitrification and functional recovery of both brain slices and in situ whole brains from adult mice. After rewarming, the tissue showed restored metabolic activity, electrophysiological function, and synaptic plasticity. This breakthrough addresses long-standing challenges in cryobiology—ice crystal formation and cryoprotectant toxicity—and brings the field significantly closer to viable long-term preservation of complex neural tissue with functional integrity. It opens new pathways for brain banking, neuroscience research, and potential future applications in cryonics. The V3 solution was used at 59% w/v concentration, and vascular perfusion balanced dehydration and cryoprotectant penetration in whole-brain preservation. Electron microscopy confirmed well-preserved ultrastructure in the hippocampal CA1 region after vitrification and rewarming.

telegram · zaihuapd · Mar 15, 08:30

**Background**: Vitrification is a cryopreservation technique that uses high concentrations of cryoprotectants and rapid cooling to transform biological tissue into a glass-like solid without forming ice crystals. Unlike traditional slow-freezing methods, vitrification avoids mechanical damage from ice, preserving cellular structure and function more effectively. It has been widely used in embryo and oocyte freezing but remains challenging for large, complex tissues like the brain due to toxicity and uneven cryoprotectant distribution.

## [Canada's Bill C-22 Mandates Mass Metadata Surveillance](https://www.michaelgeist.ca/2026/03/a-tale-of-two-bills-lawful-access-returns-with-changes-to-warrantless-access-but-dangerous-backdoor-surveillance-risks-remains/) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Canada’s Bill C-22, introduced in 2026, establishes a legal framework requiring telecom and online service providers to retain certain categories of metadata—such as transmission and location data—for up to one year and to cooperate with law enforcement access requests. While the bill claims not to introduce new surveillance powers, it enables warrant-based access with exceptions that may allow delayed or omitted notification to affected individuals. This legislation significantly impacts digital privacy rights by institutionalizing mass metadata retention for all Canadians, not just suspects, raising concerns about function creep, abuse, and compliance with Charter protections against unreasonable search and seizure. It also aligns Canada more closely with Five Eyes surveillance practices, potentially undermining public trust in digital services. The bill explicitly permits regulations mandating retention of metadata—including transmission data as defined in Section 487.011 of the Criminal Code—for up to one year. It also includes provisions allowing judges to waive the requirement to provide a copy of the warrant to the affected person, creating potential for covert surveillance.

hackernews · opengrass · Mar 15, 21:22

**Background**: Bill C-22 is part of Canada’s long-standing 'lawful access' initiative, which seeks to modernize police powers for the digital age. Previous attempts, such as Bills C-30 (2012) and C-51 (2015), faced strong public backlash over privacy concerns and were either withdrawn or amended. Metadata—data about communications like time, location, and device identifiers—is highly revealing even without content, and mandatory retention regimes have been criticized by privacy advocates and ruled disproportionate by courts in other democracies like the EU.
